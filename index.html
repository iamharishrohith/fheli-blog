Pillar I: Fully Homomorphic Encryption

Matches biometric face embeddings directly inside the ciphertext space using the CKKS scheme. Plaintext biometric vectors are never decrypted in device RAM, eliminating RAM-scraping attacks.

C_match = C_enroll ⊗ C_live

Pillar II: Passive Corneal Color-Glow

Projects multi-channel chromatic grids at 100ms intervals. Normal vector maps reconstructed from corneal reflections verify 3D curvature, instantly blocking flat paper photos and screen replays.

Pillar III: 3D Parallax Optical Flow

Utilizes natural micro-saccadic hand tremors to estimate depth. Measures optical flow velocity variance of the nose tip relative to ears, flagging and rejecting 2D flat portrait spoofs.

Defeating "Indian Jugaad" Field Cheats at Highway Construction Zones Using Edge AI

Published June 2026 | Technical Architecture Team

Deploying attendance management apps in remote, zero-network environments exposes biometric systems to extreme exploitation. When supervisor devices are offline, local caches, plain text databases, and camera frames become prime targets for manipulation. In this article, we break down how Fheli is designed to deflect the five most common field cheats (often referred to as "Indian Jugaad") attempted at construction corridors.

1. The "Photo Cutout" Hack (Eyes Cut Out)

The Cheat: A proxy worker prints a high-resolution photograph of the supervisor, cuts holes out at the eye locations, and places their own face behind the paper sheet. Standard eye-blink (EAR) algorithms detect the real eye movement behind the cutout and accept the spoof.

Fheli Deflection: Fheli solves this by analyzing depth contours. Our **3D Parallax Optical Flow Tracker** measures coordinates relative to hand tremors. Since the photo cutout lies on a flat 2D plane while the eyes sit on a separate depth layer behind it, the algorithm registers a depth velocity variance anomaly and immediately rejects the capture. Furthermore, the paper boundary absorbs the display color shifts rather than reflecting them, failing the corneal check.

2. The Live Video Call / Proxy Screen Playback

The Cheat: An absent supervisor or engineer video-calls an on-site proxy worker. The proxy worker holds the screen of their secondary phone playing the live video stream in front of the scanner device.

Fheli Deflection: During frames processing, Fheli's display changes colors (Emerald Green -> Sapphire Blue -> Ruby Red) at 100ms intervals. Because video streams introduce a network and encoding latency of at least 150ms, the reflected chromatic grid on the iris fails the real-time time-correlation test, and the login attempt is flagged as a replay attack.

3. Sibling & Relative Proxy Checks

The Cheat: Siblings or close relatives who resemble the authorized worker attempt to verify check-ins for one another.

Fheli Deflection: While normal mobile face matching algorithms have a wide tolerance, Fheli maps 468 landmarks (MediaPipe Face Mesh) at sub-pixel resolution. It computes unique ratios, such as nose bridge slope, pupil-to-pupil spacing, and outer ear alignment. The matching threshold is locked at a strict ≥ 0.95 similarity score, ensuring twin-level variations are captured.

4. Intentional Low-Light / Mud Cheats

The Cheat: Workers stand in heavy shadow zones, under umbrellas, or intentionally smear dust or mud on their cheeks, hoping the model will lower its confidence threshold and "fail open."

Fheli Deflection: Fheli utilizes dynamic exposure bracketing (using the mobile screen as a fill flash) to illuminate dark spots. More importantly, Fheli is programmed to **fail closed**. If contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE) preprocessing fails to normalize facial landmarks above ≥ 90% confidence, the authentication session terminates. The system never falls back to a lower match threshold.

5. Rooted SQLite Local Database Injection

The Cheat: A collusive project site supervisor roots the corporate mobile device to access the local database directly. They manually insert raw SQL attendance rows for absent friends.

Fheli Deflection: Fheli secures the local database using SQLCipher AES-256 encryption, locked via keys generated in the device's hardware-backed Secure Enclave. Additionally, attendance logs require a zk-SNARK cryptographic proof signature. Any database record manually inserted without generating a valid ZK-Proof on-device will fail signature verification once the logs batch-sync to AWS, triggering an immediate security audit.

Cryptographic Matching Pipeline (Homomorphic Encryption)

To ensure absolute biometric privacy, Fheli matches face embeddings directly within the homomorphic ciphertext space using the CKKS encryption scheme. The raw enrollment vector and the live capture vector are encrypted immediately on-device; the matching operation executes mathematical functions directly on these ciphertexts without decrypting them in RAM, preventing memory-scraping attacks.
